Wearable Rehabilitation · Bionics Team

Wearable Exoskeleton
Rehabilitation Device

Year 2024 – 2025
Team University of Ottawa Bionics Team
Discipline Biomedical & Mechanical Engineering
Status Prototype Complete

Overview

This project involved the design, analysis, and prototyping of a lightweight wearable lower-limb exoskeleton intended to assist stroke survivors during gait rehabilitation. The device provides passive and semi-active joint support at the knee, reducing the muscular effort required for ambulation while collecting biomechanical data to inform clinical decisions.

Developed within the University of Ottawa Bionics Team, the system integrates structural mechanical components with embedded electronics, forming a platform for real-world rehabilitation research.

Problem Statement

Post-stroke gait impairment affects over 80% of stroke survivors. Conventional physiotherapy is labour-intensive, limited in session frequency, and lacks objective, real-time biomechanical feedback. Existing commercial exoskeletons are prohibitively expensive (CAD $80,000+) and confined to clinical settings, preventing widespread accessibility.

The engineering challenge was to create a low-cost, modular, and mechanically sound wearable device that supports knee joint function during rehabilitation exercises while capturing angle and force data for analysis.

Design Approach

The design process followed a structured mechanical engineering methodology: requirements definition, concept generation, detailed CAD modelling, stress and kinematic analysis, manufacturing, and iterative testing.

A single-degree-of-freedom knee joint mechanism was designed in SolidWorks, using a four-bar linkage to approximate the polycentric motion of the human knee. The structural frame was manufactured via FDM 3D printing in PLA+, with critical load-bearing joints reinforced using aluminium inserts.

An Arduino-based embedded control system monitored joint angle via potentiometer and resistance via FSR sensors in the insole, enabling real-time gait phase detection.

Tools & Technologies

SolidWorks Arduino Python 3D Printing (FDM) C++ FSR Sensors PCB Design FEA (SimScale)

System Architecture & Design

The exoskeleton is composed of three primary subsystems:

  • Structural Frame: 3D-printed thigh and shank cuffs connected by the four-bar knee mechanism. Total weight: 780 g per limb.
  • Sensing System: Rotary potentiometer at the knee joint + three FSR sensors distributed across the insole to detect heel strike, midstance, and toe-off events.
  • Control & Data Logging: Arduino Nano processes sensor signals at 100 Hz and transmits data over USB serial to a Python data acquisition script for post-session analysis.

Results

The functional prototype successfully demonstrated passive knee support across a full gait cycle in bench-top testing. Key outcomes included:

  • Accurate gait phase detection with FSR array achieving 92% phase classification accuracy across 40 test trials.
  • Structural integrity verified under static loading to 1.5 × maximum expected body-weight force (440 N).
  • Knee angle tracking within ±2° of reference goniometer throughout range of motion.
  • Total unit cost of materials: CAD $220 — representing a >99% cost reduction versus commercial alternatives.

Lessons Learned

One of the most valuable insights was the importance of designing for manufacturing from the outset. Several early bracket iterations failed due to layer delamination in the print orientation, which was resolved by redesigning parts to align structural loads with the print direction.

Working in a multidisciplinary team also reinforced the need for clearly defined interfaces between mechanical and electrical subsystems — including toleranced mounting features for PCB components and shielded cable routing paths built into the structural frame.

Future Improvements

Several directions would strengthen this system in subsequent design iterations:

  • Integration of a brushless DC actuator at the knee joint to transition from passive to powered assistance.
  • Wireless BLE data transmission to replace the tethered USB serial link, improving clinical usability.
  • Finite element analysis refinement using patient-specific limb geometry derived from CT imaging.
  • Human subjects pilot study to validate biomechanical efficacy in collaboration with the uOttawa rehabilitation clinic.
